The Mission

Two classes in and the curriculum is already firing on multiple levels. The technical content is real ‚Äî we touched the client-server model, the three pillars of the web, semantic HTML ‚Äî but the meta-curriculum is just as heavy: how to learn, how to stay in the game, how to not fall apart when it gets hard.

The web has three pillars. HTML is the skeleton ‚Äî structure, content, the bones of everything. CSS is the skin ‚Äî it makes the bones look like something. JavaScript is the organs ‚Äî the behavior, the interactivity, the thing that makes it alive. Separation of Concerns means you keep them in separate files. Why? Organization. Readability. So when something breaks at 2am you're not drowning in one unholy file of spaghetti. (Leon would never.)

The Learning Ritual

Here's the part that broke my note-taking brain: you're not supposed to take notes. Traditional notes are a trap. Reading something, highlighting it, and feeling like you've learned it is a lie your brain tells you. The science backs this up ‚Äî go take Dr. Barbara Oakley's "Learning How to Learn" (free, Coursera, will rewire how you study everything).

Active Recall means you read something once, close the notes, and force yourself to reconstruct what you just consumed. The struggle to remember IS the learning. The friction is the point.

Spaced Repetition means you don't cram. You review at increasing intervals ‚Äî right before you'd forget it. The tool: Anki (apps.ankiweb.net). Free. The whole program can be done without spending a dollar, and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something.

The note-taking shift: instead of writing facts, write questions. "What is spaced repetition?" is a better note than "spaced repetition = reviewing at intervals." One is passive. One makes you a developer.
